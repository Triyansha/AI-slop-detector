# The Writing & Presentation Craft Reference

**Purpose:** This is the *standard* the de-slop action pulls toward. Detecting slop tells you what's wrong; this file tells you what *right* looks like, drawn from the people who defined good writing and clear communication. When de-slop rewrites a sentence, restructures a memo, or fixes a slide, it is applying a rule from this file — not improvising. Every rule here is traceable to a named source, because a de-slopper with no canon is just another generator producing fresh slop.

**The through-line:** Good writing and AI slop are near-perfect opposites. Slop is abstract, hedged, padded, passive, monotone, and generic. The entire canon below optimizes for the reverse: **concrete, committed, compressed, active, varied, and specific.** That is not a coincidence — it is why these rules are the right antidote.

## Part 1 — The Prose Canon

### Stephen King — *On Writing*

1. **"The adverb is not your friend."** Adverbs propping up weak verbs ("said softly," "walked quickly") are a crutch. Replace the verb+adverb with one strong verb.
2. **"Kill your darlings."** Cut the sentences and images you personally love if they don't serve clarity or pace. Slop is *full* of darlings — the decorative metaphor, the clever aside that says nothing.
3. **"2nd Draft = 1st Draft – 10%."** A literal formula. On revision, cut roughly a tenth of the words. This single rule kills most padding.
4. **"Write with the door closed, rewrite with the door open."** Draft for yourself; revise for the reader. De-slopping is the door-open pass.
5. **Use the first plain word, not the fancy synonym.** "Use," not "utilize." Don't dress simple thoughts in pretension.

> **Before:** "It was very clear to everyone in the small town that something was definitely wrong with the old house on the hill."
> **After:** "Everyone in town knew something was wrong with the old house on the hill." *(–10%, adverbs gone)*

### Strunk & White — *The Elements of Style*

1. **"Omit needless words."** The founding command. Every word must earn its place.
2. **"Use the active voice."** More direct, vigorous, concise than passive.
3. **"Use definite, specific, concrete language."** "A red barn," not "an agricultural structure."
4. **"Do not overstate."** Cut inflated modifiers — very, extremely, incredibly. They blur rather than intensify.
5. **"Do not explain too much."** Don't tack on a phrase restating what the sentence already says (the slop "unnecessary explainer").

> **Before:** "It is important to note that this report basically provides a general overview of the current situation as it stands."
> **After:** "This report gives an overview of the current situation."

### William Zinsser — *On Writing Well*

1. **"Clutter is the disease of American writing."** Hunt and cut: "at this point in time," "due to the fact that," "in order to."
2. **"Strip every sentence to its cleanest components."** Of every word ask: *Do I need this?*
3. **"Be yourself."** Avoid pompous, bureaucratic voice. Write the words you'd actually say.
4. **Abstract nouns → people doing things.** Slop loves "the optimization of workflows." Humans say "if we simplify the workflow, the team works faster."

> **Before:** "Errors were made in the implementation phase that resulted in a suboptimal user experience."
> **After:** "We made mistakes during the build and hurt the user experience." *(passive → active, abstraction → people)*

### George Orwell — "Politics and the English Language"

Orwell's six rules are themselves a near-complete anti-slop checklist:

1. Never use a **metaphor or figure of speech you're used to seeing in print** (no dead clichés).
2. Never use a **long word** where a short one will do (utilize → use, facilitate → help).
3. If it is possible to **cut a word**, cut it.
4. Never use the **passive** where you can use the active.
5. Never use a **jargon word or foreign phrase** if an everyday English equivalent exists.
6. **Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous.** Clarity beats rule-following.

> **Before:** "It has been determined that the implementation of this initiative will facilitate the optimization of productivity metrics across the organization."
> **After:** "We decided this project will help the company get more done."

### Roy Peter Clark — *Writing Tools*

1. **Strong verbs drive strong sentences.** Replace "to be" verbs and verb+adverb pairs with vivid action verbs. ("walked slowly" → "crept")
2. **Cut big, then small.** First remove whole redundant sections; then trim sentences and words.
3. **Vary sentence length** — rhythm and music come from mixing short and long. Slop is metronomic; this is the direct fix for it.
4. **Favor simple subject–verb–object order**, especially at the start of a piece or paragraph.

> **Monotone (slop):** "Our team worked hard. We faced challenges. We did our best. We learned a lot."
> **Varied (Clark):** "Our team worked hard. We hit walls — budget cuts, shifting priorities, an outage at the worst possible time. We pushed through anyway, and learned more than we expected."

### Ann Handley — *Everybody Writes*

1. **Write to one person, not "an audience."** Generic tone is the signature of slop.
2. **Be specific, not vague.** Swap buzzwords for concrete detail.
3. **Ditch the robot voice.** Conversational phrasing, contractions, real examples.
4. **Make it useful.** Cut anything that doesn't help the reader do or understand something.

> **Before:** "Our innovative, cutting-edge solution empowers businesses to leverage synergies and unlock unprecedented value."
> **After:** "Our software helps small teams track projects in one place, so they miss fewer deadlines."

## Part 2 — The Clarity & Structure Canon (business writing)

### Joseph Williams — *Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace*

The two most powerful clarity principles in all of business writing:

1. **Characters as subjects, actions as verbs.** Prose reads clearly when the *subject names who's acting* and the *verb names the action*. The enemy is the **nominalization** — a verb frozen into a noun ("make a decision" instead of "decide," "perform an analysis" instead of "analyze").
   > **Before:** "An evaluation of the campaign was conducted, and a decision regarding continuation was made."
   > **After:** "The marketing team evaluated the campaign and decided to continue it."

2. **Old-before-new (cohesion).** Begin sentences with *familiar* information; end with the *new* or important information. This is what makes paragraphs feel inevitable instead of choppy — and AI slop is choppy because it ignores it.
   > **Choppy:** "We will launch an AI pilot in Q3. Support tickets rose 30% last year."
   > **Cohesive:** "Support tickets rose 30% last year. Because of that increase, we will launch an AI pilot in Q3."

3. **Core clause early, heavy detail late.** Don't wedge long modifiers between subject and verb.
   > **Before:** "Our team, following extensive discussions over three quarters, has concluded we should sunset the API."
   > **After:** "Our team has concluded we should sunset the API, after three quarters of discussion."

### The Economist Style Guide — concreteness & accountability

1. **Short, concrete words over pompous synonyms.** If a smart lay reader wouldn't use the word, replace it.
2. **Active voice for ownership.** Passive hides the actor; name who decided and who owns it.
3. **No jargon or clichés.** Test: *if this phrase could appear in any company's deck this year, cut it.* ("move the needle," "think outside the box.")
4. **Be numerate and bounded.** Replace "strong growth" with "8–10% revenue growth." Numbers, dates, and thresholds turn fog into a plan — and are exactly what slop's "fabricated precision" only *pretends* to do.

### Barbara Minto — The Pyramid Principle & BLUF

1. **Bottom Line Up Front.** Lead with the answer in one sentence, then justify. The reader should get the recommendation from the first line.
   > **Buried:** "As you know, we've been exploring various options to improve resilience…"
   > **BLUF:** "We recommend migrating to AWS within 12 months to cut downtime risk in half."

2. **Group support into 3–4 logical reasons, not a flat list of facts.** Top = the answer; next level = the key arguments; under each = evidence.

3. **Every section answers an explicit question the reader is asking.** "Background" is generic; "Why on-prem can't meet our 24/7 uptime target" is specific.

4. **Parallel structure for comparable items** — the same metrics at the same level of detail, so trade-offs are visible. (Directly fixes the slop "options table with no real comparison.")

## Part 3 — The Presentation & Visual Canon

### Nancy Duarte — *Resonate*

1. **Define the change in the audience first.** Before any slide, state what people should *think, feel, or do differently* afterward. ("Skeptical managers leave willing to pilot the workflow in one team next quarter.") This kills the informational-sludge deck.
2. **"What is" vs. "What could be" contrast.** Oscillate between current reality and the better alternative to create tension that makes the recommendation feel necessary.
3. **Slides support the spoken story; they don't duplicate it.** The deck shows the one thing to remember; your voice carries the nuance. (Fixes the slop "speaker notes that are just a transcript.")
4. **Concrete anecdote over abstract claim.** "A customer waited 11 days because no one owned the ticket" beats "this will increase accountability."

### Garr Reynolds — *Presentation Zen*

1. **Plan in analog, end in mind.** Sketch on paper before opening the slide tool — it stops you defaulting to the template and the bullet wall.
2. **One main idea per slide.** If it can't be grasped in ~3 seconds, it's overloaded. Split it.
3. **Remove visual clutter; let white space work.** Ornament reads as *less* confident, not more.
4. **Talk *with* peers, not *at* them.** Spoken-language slide titles ("Why we're missing our targets," not "Performance Deficit Drivers").

### Edward Tufte — data display

1. **Maximize the data-ink ratio.** Erase any ink that isn't data — 3D bars, heavy gridlines, decorative backgrounds, drop shadows.
2. **Show data variation, not design variation.** All bars one neutral color; the one that matters in a single accent. The story becomes "this bar differs," not "this slide has a palette."
3. **Label directly on the graphic**, with units. An exec should grasp the chart without narration.
4. **Never distort the quantitative story.** Start bar axes at zero; keep true proportions. In business, this is the line between persuasion and manipulation.

## Part 4 — The De-Slop Transformation Rules (the operational layer)

Each rule maps a detected slop tell to a concrete rewrite move, traceable to the canon above. This is what `deslop.md` executes.

| # | Transformation | Source | The move |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Cut filler phrases** | Strunk, Zinsser, Orwell | "in order to"→"to"; "due to the fact that"→"because"; delete "it is important to note that" |
| 2 | **Passive → active** | Strunk, Orwell, Williams | Find "to-be + past participle"; if an actor exists or is inferable, flip to subject–verb–object |
| 3 | **Weak verb + adverb → strong verb** | King, Clark | "walked slowly"→"crept"; "said quietly"→"whispered" |
| 4 | **Delete hedging** | King, Handley | Cut "somewhat / rather / it seems / in many ways / I believe that" unless genuinely load-bearing |
| 5 | **Abstraction/jargon → specifics** | Orwell, Zinsser, Economist | Ask "who does what, to whom, with what result?"; "improve efficiency"→"the team finishes work faster" |
| 6 | **Un-nominalize** | Williams | "make a decision"→"decide"; "perform an analysis"→"analyze" |
| 7 | **Split overlong sentences** | Clark, King | Sentences >30 words with stacked clauses → one core sentence + one supporting |
| 8 | **Vary rhythm** | Clark | Break a run of same-length, same-opening sentences ("Furthermore… Moreover… Additionally…") |
| 9 | **Kill clichés & dead metaphors** | Orwell | "at the end of the day," "move the needle," "paradigm shift" → cut or replace with a fresh, concrete image |
| 10 | **Apply the –10% cut** | King | Target a 10% word reduction; surface the lowest-information sentences for deletion |
| 11 | **Old → new cohesion** | Williams | Reorder so each sentence opens from known info and ends on the new point |
| 12 | **Bottom line up front** | Minto | Move the recommendation to the first sentence/slide; justify after |
| 13 | **Add numbers & bounds** | Economist | Replace "strong growth" with a range, date, or threshold — *only if the user supplies the real figure* (else flag a gap) |
| 14 | **One idea per slide** | Reynolds, Duarte | Split overloaded slides; rewrite titles into spoken-language claims |
| 15 | **Strip chartjunk** | Tufte | Remove non-data ink; one accent color; direct labels; zero-baseline bars |

### The cardinal rule of de-slopping: **never fabricate to fill a gap.**

Rules 5 and 13 frequently want a specific the model cannot know — a real metric, a named source, an actual customer quote. The de-slopper **must not invent one.** It inserts a visible flag — `⚠️ [GAP: insert the real Q3 number]` — and tells the human exactly what to supply. A rewrite that invents a plausible "$2.3M" to replace "significant value" has not removed the slop; it has *upgraded* it into something more dangerous. Restraint here is the entire quality bar.

## Part 5 — The Master Standard

Every rewrite is checked against the same nine questions the best editors apply (a fusion of the canon above):

1. **Characters & actions** — is who-did-what the subject and verb?
2. **Old → new** — does each sentence open from the known and end on the new?
3. **Bottom line up front** — can a busy reader get the point from the first line?
4. **Specifics over generalities** — are "improve/optimize/enhance" replaced with numbers, names, examples?
5. **Active ownership** — are decision-makers and owners named, not hidden in passive voice?
6. **Parallel structure** — are options described in the same format so trade-offs show?
7. **Rhythm** — do sentence lengths and openings vary?
8. **Compression** — has ~10% been cut? Does every word earn its place?
9. **No fabrication** — is every specific real, with gaps flagged rather than filled?

If a rewrite passes these nine, it has left slop behind and arrived at writing the canon would recognize as good.
