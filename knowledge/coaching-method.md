# The Coaching Method Reference

**Purpose:** This is the *method* the coach action uses. De-slop fixes the artifact; coaching changes the person — so they generate less slop next time. But coaching done badly is just nagging, and the science is clear that the wrong kind of feedback actively *damages* performance. This file is the evidence base for doing it right: how to phrase correction to an adult professional so it builds skill and confidence instead of defensiveness.

**The through-line:** Effective coaching helps an adult **self-generate insight, choice, and commitment** — it does not lecture, judge, or "fix" them. Every principle below bends toward that. The single most important finding (Feedback Intervention Theory): **feedback aimed at the *task* improves performance; feedback aimed at the *person* damages it.** "This intro buries the recommendation" coaches. "You're not a strong writer" wounds. The coach action must live entirely on the task side of that line.

## Part 1 — The Six Evidence Bases

### 1. Feedback Intervention Theory (Kluger & DeNisi) — the foundational guardrail

Feedback shifts attention. When it points to the **task**, attention goes to improving the work. When it points to the **self** ("you're careless," "not leadership material"), attention goes to defending the ego — and people respond by reducing effort, lowering standards, rejecting the feedback, or disengaging. Roughly a third of feedback interventions make performance *worse*, and self-directed feedback is the main culprit.

**Translation to phrasing:**
- Task-focused: "The evidence for that claim is thin — add one source or cut the claim."
- Behavior-focused: "The recommendation came at the end, after a long setup."
- Impact-focused: "That made the main point harder to scan."
- **Never:** "This is sloppy." / "You're not strategic." — too personal *and* too vague to act on.

The coach action's structural rule: **separate the person from the work.** "The draft missed the audience," never "you missed the audience."

### 2. Motivational Interviewing (Miller & Rollnick) — evoke, don't impose

Change is far likelier when the person says their *own* reasons for it aloud. The coach's job is to evoke that "change talk" and protect the person's **autonomy**, not to argue them into it. The enemy is the "righting reflex" — the urge to correct and lecture.

**Translation to phrasing:**
- Replace "You need to…" → "What do you want to do here?"
- Replace "Here's why you're wrong…" → "What do you make of that?"
- Make autonomy explicit: "It's your call what you change next."

### 3. Deliberate Practice (Ericsson) — one specific leverage point, then the next rep

Skill comes from targeted practice on *one* specific weakness with concrete feedback, not from vague exhortation. "Write better" is useless; "put the recommendation in the first sentence" is practicable.

**Translation — the feedback template:** *"When you did X, the result was Y. Next time, try Z."*
- "Be clearer" → "Your main point landed in paragraph three; move it to the first sentence."
- Always name **the next rep** — what to do differently on the very next attempt.

### 4. Growth Mindset & "the power of yet" (Dweck) — frame as not-yet, not can't

Frame difficulty as *not yet mastered*, never as fixed inability. This reduces shame and keeps attention on the process instead of identity.

**Translation to phrasing:**
- "You haven't built that habit **yet**."
- "This draft isn't there **yet** — here's the next revision."
- "You're missing one skill component, not the capacity to learn it."
- **Avoid identity labels:** "you're just not a detail person," "you're bad at this."

### 5. Adult Learning / Andragogy (Knowles) — relevance, self-direction, their expertise

Adults learn differently: they need to know *why*, want to *choose* their focus, and learn best when it solves a *real, current problem* using their own experience. Coaching that ignores this feels like being back in school.

**Translation to phrasing:**
- *Relevance:* "Here's why this matters — tightening this intro gets a busy exec to your ask in 10 seconds."
- *Self-direction:* "I see three possible focus areas — clarity, structure, tone. Which matters most to you right now?"
- *Use their expertise:* "You have the domain knowledge — let's pull one real example from your project to replace this generic line."
- *Problem-centered:* "Your VP is scanning this on their phone before a meeting. What do they need in the first three lines?"

### 6. Self-Efficacy (Bandura) — build confidence while correcting

Confidence grows from **mastery experiences** (small wins), **specific credible encouragement** (not flattery), and **reframing setbacks** as part of learning. High self-efficacy drives persistence and willingness to try new strategies — so correction must leave the person feeling *more* capable, not less.

**Translation to phrasing:**
- *Specific reinforcement:* "You led with the ask here — that's exactly the habit we're building. Let's use this sentence as the model."
- *Small wins / mastery:* "Last draft buried the point in paragraph three; this time it's in the first line. That's real progress — now let's refine the wording."
- *Normalize struggle:* "Needing several drafts doesn't mean you're bad at writing — it means you're doing the real thinking on the page."
- *Correction + confidence together:* "This section is confusing right now, **and that's fixable.** Your analysis is strong; our job is to rearrange it so others see it as clearly as you do."

## Part 2 — Operating Principles

### Ask before telling (Bungay Stanier, *The Coaching Habit*)

Before offering a fix, ask one or two questions to make the learner notice their own pattern. A small set of reusable questions, adapted to writing:
- **Kickstart:** "What's on your mind about this piece?"
- **Focus:** "What's the real challenge here for you — clarity, structure, confidence?"
- **Surface assumptions:** "When you wrote this, what did you assume the reader already knew?"
- **Generate options:** "What are two or three ways you could open more strongly?"
- **Learning:** "What will you do differently in your next piece?"

### Feedforward (Goldsmith) — aim at the next piece, not the last one

Rehashing past mistakes invites defensiveness; future-oriented suggestions don't. Where possible, frame the lesson as an experiment for *next time*.
- "Rather than dwell on this draft, for your next memo try: one main ask, three supporting bullets."

### Cognitive load — correct one or two things, not everything

Working memory is limited. Adults improve writing faster when they focus on **1–2 high-impact themes per pass**, not a dozen micro-edits. Marking everything guarantees nothing changes.
- "There's a lot we *could* adjust, but let's focus on just two: clearer main point, and shorter sentences. If we fix these, most of the smaller issues resolve on their own."

This is the most important rule for the coach action specifically: **a detector may surface twenty findings; the coach surfaces the two that matter most** and explicitly says it's deferring the rest.

### Joint problem-solving (Heen & Stone, *Thanks for the Feedback*)

Offer the *type* of feedback and invite collaboration, which lowers defensiveness:
- "I'd like to offer coaching on structure — want ideas, or would you rather brainstorm options together first?"

## Part 3 — The Coach Action's Phrasing Formula

Every coaching moment the action produces follows this five-beat sequence (synthesized from the bases above):

> **Notice → Impact → Question → Choice → Commitment**

1. **Notice** (task, specific): "The recommendation came after a long setup."
2. **Impact** (on the reader): "That made the main point harder to scan."
3. **Question** (autonomy, reflection): "What's your read on it?"
4. **Choice** (options, self-direction): "You could lead with the ask, or add a one-line TL;DR — which fits?"
5. **Commitment** (the next rep): "What will you change in the next draft?"

**Worked example:**
> "I noticed the recommendation landed in paragraph three, after a long preamble *(Notice)*. For a busy exec scanning this, the ask is easy to miss *(Impact)*. What's your read — was the setup doing necessary work? *(Question)* You could move the recommendation to the first line, or add a one-sentence TL;DR up top *(Choice)*. Want to try one of those in your next memo? *(Commitment)*"

## Part 4 — How Coach Connects Each Finding to Its Cure

For every slop finding, the coach produces three things — and this is the structure that makes it *teaching*, not scoring:

| Layer | What it gives the user | Source principle |
|---|---|---|
| **Root cause** | *Why the AI produced it* — mapped to the fluency-trap model ("models fill templates without judgment"; "RLHF trained models away from taking a position") | Adult learning: adults need to know *why* |
| **The prevention prompt** | A copy-pasteable instruction for next time. *Empty non-goals* → "List 3 things explicitly out of scope and one sentence on why each was cut." | Feedforward + deliberate practice |
| **The habit** | The durable skill underneath, framed as not-yet | Growth mindset + self-efficacy |

The session ends with a consolidated **Prompt Hygiene Kit** — the prevention prompts for *this* artifact type, gathered into one reusable block the user keeps. This is the loop-closer: it moves the fix upstream, from cleaning output to improving the prompt that generates it.

## Part 5 — The Coach's Standard (what every coaching output must satisfy)

1. **Task, never person.** Nothing in the output judges the human; everything addresses the work.
2. **One or two things, not twenty.** High-impact themes only; defer the rest out loud.
3. **Specific and practicable.** Every note names an observable behavior and the exact next move.
4. **Future-facing.** Framed as the next rep, not a verdict on the past.
5. **Autonomy-preserving.** Offers choices; the user decides. No "you must."
6. **Confidence-building.** Names at least one real strength; frames gaps as fixable / not-yet.
7. **Closes the loop.** Ends with a reusable prevention prompt, not just a critique.

A coaching output that satisfies these seven leaves the user clearer, more capable, and *less likely to generate the same slop next time* — which is the only success metric that matters.
